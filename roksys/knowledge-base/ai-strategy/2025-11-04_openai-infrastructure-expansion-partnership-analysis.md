## Summary

- OpenAI announced a $38 billion deal with Amazon AWS for cloud computing services over 7 years, adding to a massive infrastructure buildout totaling over $1 trillion in commitments
- Despite aggressive expansion and revenue claims of 'well above' $13 billion annually, OpenAI posted an $11.5 billion loss in the recent quarter and has yet to turn a profit
- The company is using diverse deal structures to avoid immediate cash outlays while maintaining a 'too big to fail' narrative ahead of potential IPO
- Strategic partnerships include Oracle ($400B Stargate), Nvidia ($100B), AMD ($100M), Oracle ($300B), and Broadcom, diversifying beyond Microsoft dependency
- Minor story: Google removed Gemma model due to hallucinations (mentioned in title but not detailed in content)

## Key Insights

- OpenAI's deal-making strategy appears focused on building a 'too big to fail' ecosystem by tying major tech companies' fortunes to its own success, reducing single-vendor dependency
- The company is using creative deal structures and deferred payment arrangements to fund massive infrastructure expansion without immediate cash requirements
- Despite unprecedented scale of commitments and revenue growth, the $11.5 billion quarterly loss raises questions about path to profitability and sustainability of the current growth model
- The AI infrastructure buildout represents a major opportunity for cloud providers, chipmakers, and data center operators, creating a new battleground for market share
- OpenAI's strategy of maintaining high-profile partnerships and expansion narrative may be designed to sustain valuation momentum until IPO, but requires eventual revenue realization

‚ÄúAI infrastructure continues to be a key battleground for growth, which benefits cloud providers, chipmakers, and data center operators that can meet the demand,‚Äù Ido Caspi, research analyst at Global X, told The Deep View. ‚ÄúThe move also reflects OpenAI‚Äôs strategic effort to diversify its dependencies beyond a single vendor in Microsoft.‚Äù¬†

But for all of its pomp and circumstance, OpenAI has yet to turn a profit. ‚ÄúThey're building this hype cycle narrative, and it's driving their ability to raise funds and their ability to do big deals,‚Äù said Bickley. ‚ÄúThey‚Äôre keeping this narrative going between now and IPO, that‚Äôs the key strategy.‚Äù

# Microsoft racks up compute deals

View image: (https://media.beehiiv.com/cdn-cgi/image/fit=scale-down,format=auto,onerror=redirect,quality=80/uploads/asset/file/27852568-1bde-4680-92f7-b0d843745bfb/ART2__0-00-00-00___50_.jpg?t=1762259619)
Caption: 

Microsoft is shelling out for AI power.¬†

The tech giant announced a swathe of deals and partnerships on Monday, largely aimed at boosting its AI infrastructure and cloud capacity as competitors rapidly forge billions worth of data center deals.¬†

With all of these deals, Nvidia‚Äôs chips are the common denominator:¬†

* Microsoft signed a $9.7 billion deal with Australian [cloud computing firm IREN](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2025/11/03/3178993/0/en/IREN-Secures-9-7bn-AI-Cloud-Contract-with-Microsoft.html) on Monday. The partnership provides Microsoft access to Nvidia‚Äôs GB300 AI architecture.

* Separately, Microsoft [announced a deal with cloud firm Lambda](https://lambda.ai/blog/lambda-announces-multibillion-dollar-agreement-with-microsoft-to-deploy-ai-infrastructure-powered-by-tens-of-thousands-of-nvidia-gpus) on Monday worth billions of dollars and powered by ‚Äútens of thousands‚Äù of Nvidia GPUs.¬†

* The company also announced further investment in AI capacity in the United Arab Emirates, [totalling $15.2 billion by 2029](https://blogs.microsoft.com/on-the-issues/2025/11/03/microsofts-15-2-billion-usd-investment-in-the-uae/). More than $5.5 billion of this investment will go towards AI and cloud infrastructure, and the investment will allow advanced Nvidia GPUs to be shipped into the country.¬†

Inking all of these deals, it‚Äôs clear that Microsoft is feeling the pressure to build out its compute capacity as [competitors pour billions](https://www.thedeepview.com/articles/oracle-softbank-openai-power-stargate-expansion) into rapidly deploying infrastructure.¬†

The company noted during its earnings call last week that it's planning to [double its data center footprint](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/microsoft-msft-q1-earnings-report-2026-b342eefd?mod=article_inline) over the next two years as demand for its cloud business spikes. To meet demand, Microsoft may be eyeing alternatives, taking a [particular interest in neoclouds](https://www.thedeepview.com/articles/nscale-scores-microsoft-deal-hints-at-ipo) and in cloud infrastructure purpose-built for AI workloads in recent months.¬†

But these partnerships underscore that, even as companies seek alternatives to Nvidia ‚Äì such as [Oracle‚Äôs partnership with AMD](https://www.thedeepview.com/newsletter/amd-oracle-partnership-highlights-nvidia-rivalry) or [Anthropic‚Äôs with Google](https://www.thedeepview.com/newsletter/anthropic-google-partner-on-tpus) ‚Äì its chips are still far and away the leader of the pack.

# Google removes Gemma after hallucinations

View image: (https://media.beehiiv.com/cdn-cgi/image/fit=scale-down,format=auto,onerror=redirect,quality=80/uploads/asset/file/80cdd2dc-6a81-4169-b4dc-35f34685c37e/ART3__0-00-00-00___47_.jpg?t=1762259636)
Caption: 

AI models still have trouble separating fact from fiction.¬†

Google on Friday said it pulled Gemma from its AI studio platform after Republican Sen. Marsha Blackburn [penned a letter](https://www.blackburn.senate.gov/services/files/5651166C-7B30-4BA0-9E86-F3DD9521CA00) to the company accusing the model of fabricating accusations of sexual misconduct against her.¬†

In Blackburn‚Äôs letter to Google CEO Sundar Pichai last week, she claims that when prompting Gemma with ‚ÄúHas Marsha Blackburn been accused of rape,‚Äù the model responds with claims that she had a relationship with a state trooper involving ‚Äúnon-consensual acts‚Äù during her 1987 campaign, which she notes is untrue.¬†

‚ÄúThis is not a harmless ‚Äúhallucination.‚Äù It is an act of defamation produced and distributed by a Google-owned AI model,‚Äù Blackburn writes. ‚ÄúA publicly accessible tool that invents false criminal allegations about a sitting U.S. Senator represents a catastrophic failure of oversight and ethical responsibility.‚Äù¬†

Google responded in a tweet saying that Gemma in AI studio was never intended to be a consumer tool capable of answering ‚Äúfactual questions.‚Äù The company noted that it remains ‚Äúcommitted to minimizing hallucinations and continually improving all our models.‚Äù¬†

Tweet:  (https://twitter.com/NewsFromGoogle/status/1984412632913494456)

It‚Äôs not the first time we‚Äôve seen hallucinations bubble up in the news: In early October, Deloitte had to refund the Australian government for [a report littered with AI hallucinations](https://arstechnica.com/ai/2025/10/deloitte-will-refund-australian-government-for-ai-hallucination-filled-report/). And in May, Anthropic‚Äôs lawyers filed documentation with a hallucinated footnote in the AI firm‚Äôs [legal battle with music publishers](https://www.reuters.com/legal/legalindustry/anthropics-lawyers-take-blame-ai-hallucination-music-publishers-lawsuit-2025-05-15/).¬†

Hallucinations come about when models answer questions on topics outside of the data they were trained on, thereby making up answers. One solution to limiting hallucinations is allowing models to simply abstain from answering questions they don‚Äôt know, as [proposed in an OpenAI paper](https://openai.com/index/why-language-models-hallucinate/) published in September.¬†

Still, despite efforts to curtail hallucinations, actually getting rid of them is likely impossible. Though Blackburn‚Äôs letter calls on Google to ‚ÄúShut it down until you can control it,‚Äù the question of how much hallucination AI users can tolerate remains unanswered.
