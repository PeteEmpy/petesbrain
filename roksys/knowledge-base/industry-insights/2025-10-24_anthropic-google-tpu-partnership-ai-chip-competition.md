# Anthropic, Google partner on TPUs 

View image: (https://media.beehiiv.com/cdn-cgi/image/fit=scale-down,format=auto,onerror=redirect,quality=80/uploads/asset/file/9d7ab6ce-7659-433b-8118-117505db3156/ART1__0-00-00-00___40_.jpg?t=1761301332)

Anthropic isn‚Äôt putting all of its eggs in one basket.¬†

The AI firm announced [a cloud partnership with Google](https://www.anthropic.com/news/expanding-our-use-of-google-cloud-tpus-and-services), giving it access to more than one million of the search giant‚Äôs Tensor Processing Units, or [TPUs](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eyAjbgkBdjU). ‚ÄúAnthropic and Google have a longstanding partnership and this latest expansion will help us continue to grow the compute we need to define the frontier of AI,‚Äù Krishna Rao, CFO of Anthropic, said in the announcement.¬†

The deal highlights that Anthropic is spreading the love amongst several different chip firms to build its AI empire. The company noted in its press release that it primarily relies on three kinds of chips: Google‚Äôs TPUs, Amazon‚Äôs Trainium, and NVIDIA‚Äôs GPUs.¬†

AMD, similarly, has [put up a fight against Nvidia‚Äôs chip dominance](https://archive.thedeepview.com/p/amd-oracle-partnership-highlights-nvidia-rivalry), announcing a partnership with Oracle last week to deploy 50,000 of its chips in data centers starting next year.

Anthropic‚Äôs play is smart. By spreading its bets across several different chipmakers, it‚Äôs not going all-in on the Nvidia bandwagon as demand for its chips soars to new heights. Portioning its workloads among different chip providers allows it to strike a better balance between cost and performance than it would if it were locked into a single vendor. # Alibaba targets consumer market

View image: (https://media.beehiiv.com/cdn-cgi/image/fit=scale-down,format=auto,onerror=redirect,quality=80/uploads/asset/file/06e9e622-70e6-484d-b07e-69ea032582e3/ART2__0-00-00-00___41_.jpg?t=1761301364)
Caption: 

Alibaba may be fighting for the consumer AI market.¬†

The Chinese tech giant launched a free-to-use chatbot on Thursday, integrated into its Quark app, the company‚Äôs primary consumer app. The move puts it in direct competition with other Chinese tech firms like Bytedance and Tencent, and marks a stark shift from the enterprise AI focus it‚Äôs taken thus far.¬†

The chatbot isn‚Äôt Alibaba‚Äôs first stab at the consumer space. The company launched an app called Tongyi in 2023, which [largely flopped in comparison](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/alibaba-launches-ai-chatbot-service-renewed-consumer-push-2025-10-23/) to offerings by other tech firms in the country.¬†

The release highlights a growing interest in capturing the consumer AI market. And as the company seeks to keep up with the massive infrastructure race by pouring roughly [$53 billion into its AI development over the next three years](https://archive.thedeepview.com/p/alibaba-joins-ai-infrastructure-race), Alibaba could be looking for ways to siphon returns.¬†

It‚Äôs an interest reflected in similar moves by U.S. firms: On Thursday, Amazon (which targets several markets similar to Alibaba's in ecommerce and cloud) rolled out its own consumer-focused AI tool called [‚ÄúHelp Me Decide,‚Äù](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-10-23/amazon-launches-new-ai-shopping-tool-in-us-for-the-indecisive?srnd=phx-technology) which recommends products to indecisive shoppers.¬†

And Alibaba might have a leg up over U.S. firms, as the consumer market may be easier to capture in China than in the U.S.: According to Stanford University‚Äôs [Human-Centered AI index report](https://hai.stanford.edu/ai-index/2025-ai-index-report), released in June, while global optimism around AI is growing, the belief that AI can do more good than harm is far higher in China than it is in the U.S., sitting at 83% and 39%, respectively.

Caption: 

It‚Äôs been a big week for robotaxis.

On Thursday, Neolix, a Chinese manufacturer developing unmanned delivery vehicles, [announced a $600 million funding round](https://asia.nikkei.com/business/technology/chinese-robovan-startup-neolix-raises-600m-for-tech-product-development). The company will invest the proceeds into developing its algorithm and hardware.¬†

Neolix is just the latest of several robotaxi firms making moves this week, signaling increasing interest in the space as AI weaves its way through every industry, including traditional service jobs.¬†

* On Wednesday, Uber and Nebius committed up to [$375 million to the development of Avride](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-10-22/uber-nebius-to-invest-up-to-375-million-in-robotaxi-firm), Nebius‚Äôs autonomous vehicle subsidiary. Avride also announced [a partnership with Grubhub](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/grubhub-expands-partnership-with-avride-to-bring-autonomous-delivery-robots-to-its-marketplace-302592083.html) on Thursday to expand robotic food delivery.¬†

* On Tesla‚Äôs [earnings call on Wednesday](https://www.theverge.com/transportation/804972/tesla-robotaxi-safety-monitor-remove-austin-musk), CEO Elon Musk promised to remove safety monitors from its robotaxis and launch in up to ten new markets by the end of 2025.¬†

* On Tuesday, Chinese self-driving firm Zelostech announced an [additional $100 million](https://www.dealstreetasia.com/stories/zelostech-ant-group-led-funding-460525) in funding led by Alibaba-owned Ant Group.¬†¬†

* And late last week, Stellantis [announced a partnership with Pony AI](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-10-17/stellantis-china-s-pony-ai-to-test-autonomous-vans-in-europe) to start testing out autonomous vans in Luxembourg, laying the groundwork for its own robotaxi service.

Investment, development and deal activity might be trending alongside consumer attention. Waymo, for example, reached [10 million robotaxi trips back in May](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/05/20/waymo-ceo-tekedra-mawakana-10-million.html), doubling its total rides in a five-month span.¬†
