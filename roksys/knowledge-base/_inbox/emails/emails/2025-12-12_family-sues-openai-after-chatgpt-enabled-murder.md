# OpenAI‚Äôs GPT-5.2 is _really_ good at spreadsheets

OpenAI rolled out an upgraded version of ChatGPT following a turbulent week in which it [declared](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/openais-altman-declares-code-red-to-improve-chatgpt-as-google-threatens-ai-lead-7faf5ea6?gaa_at=eafs&gaa_n=AWEtsqdRCqcQHItxF3N4IBEKVOixFhTRLMvoz-QgdFxolh9xwEwbfYqU5aSkcFcsuqA%3D&gaa_ts=693b2f9b&gaa_sig=LBRhSey96JrUjwMFbATynGX78p6Xbq-SylLZPtnfQRDrhwh3Id8L6AkjDMuJitingRa2g4AXQ8DsPApiKwUkmw%3D%3D) a ‚Äúcode red‚Äù in light of recent advances by Google Gemini.

# Disney chooses sides in the _AI war_

View image: (https://media.beehiiv.com/cdn-cgi/image/fit=scale-down,format=auto,onerror=redirect,quality=80/uploads/asset/file/45fe39b5-4f77-4896-8178-be3b01752e53/ART2__8_.png?t=1765539636)
Caption: 

As Hollywood debates where AI fits in, Disney has chosen which horse it wants to back.¬†

On Thursday, the entertainment giant announced a [licensing deal with OpenAI](https://thewaltdisneycompany.com/the-walt-disney-company-and-openai-reach-landmark-agreement-to-bring-beloved-characters-from-across-disneys-brands-to-sora/), allowing its video generation platform Sora to generate shorts based on more than 200 characters from its canon, including from Disney, Marvel, Pixar and Star Wars IP. Certain Sora shorts will be available to stream on Disney+ as part of the agreement.

Also, as part of the deal, Disney will become a ‚Äúmajor customer‚Äù of OpenAI, integrating its tools to build products and experiences and deploying ChatGPT to its employees. Disney will also make a $1 billion equity investment in the AI firm.¬†

Iger told CNBC on Thursday that the deal ‚Äúdoes not in any way [represent a threat to the creators](https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/business/business-news/bob-iger-openai-deal-paramount-netflix-warner-bros-1236448029/) at all‚Äù as it excludes access to likenesses and character voices.

In placing its bets on OpenAI, Disney immediately made an enemy of another AI giant: Google. On Wednesday night, the company[ sent Google a cease-and-desist letter](https://ca.news.yahoo.com/disney-sends-cease-desist-letter-155552950.html%5C), accusing the company of AI-based copyright infringement on a ‚Äúmassive scale.‚Äù Disney has sent similar cease-and-desist letters to Meta and [Character.AI](https://Character.AI) and has filed joint litigation with NBCUniversal and Warner Bros. Discover against [Midjourney](https://news.justia.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/Disney-Universal-v.-Midjourney-Complaint.pdf) and [Minimax](https://variety.com/2025/digital/news/disney-warner-bros-discovery-nbcu-lawsuit-minimax-chinese-ai-company-1236520395/).¬†

‚ÄúDisney isn't playing around. By partnering with OpenAI, they're picking someone they can work with while setting the standard for how Hollywood deals with AI,‚Äù Darrell White, partner at law firm Kimura London & White, told The Deep View. ‚ÄúThe takeaway is simple: AI is coming to entertainment, but only if the studios call the shots.‚Äù

Disney picking its winner is another signal that the industry is choosing sides as Google rapidly catches up to OpenAI. Reports emerged last week that OpenAI CEO Sam Altman signaled a ‚Äúcode red‚Äù alert to employees, demanding all hands on deck to improve its flagship chatbot. Altman also warned staff in late November that Google‚Äôs success with Gemini would [cause economic headwinds](https://www.theinformation.com/articles/openai-ceo-braces-possible-economic-headwinds-catching-resurgent-google?utm_source=thedeepview&utm_medium=newsletter&utm_campaign=google-is-making-openai-nervous).

Getting the endorsement of one of the biggest players in entertainment (not to mention debuting GPT-5.2) gives OpenAI another leg to stand on, as [Google‚Äôs gains have made it sweat](https://archive.thedeepview.com/p/google-is-making-openai-nervous).

# Companies turn _bearish on AI _adoption, based on fear

View image: (https://media.beehiiv.com/cdn-cgi/image/fit=scale-down,format=auto,onerror=redirect,quality=80/uploads/asset/file/29fa9d13-4c4a-4d22-afca-7fe5ee18f9eb/ART3__9_.png?t=1765539654)
Caption: 

At the AI Summit in New York City, technology leaders from AstraZeneca, Nvidia, the United Nations, and smaller firms debated how deeply AI should shape organizational decision-making, from internal corporate workflows to humanitarian operations.

Their consensus: AI can help organizations move faster and work smarter, but fear of risk is slowing adoption.

‚ÄúVery few clients want the whole process automated,‚Äù Graham Wilkinson, Chief Innovation Officer at Acxiom, a data services firm powering AI-driven marketing, said during the December 10 panel.

While 68% of business leaders view AI as an opportunity, the share who see it as a risk has doubled since 2024, according to a [2025 survey from Gallagher,](https://www.ajg.com/news-and-insights/features/2025-attitudes-to-ai-adoption-and-risk-benchmarking-survey/) a risk management firm. A separate [EY study ](https://www.ey.com/en_gl/insights/ai/how-can-responsible-ai-bridge-the-gap-between-investment-and-impact)from this year found that companies lost an average of 4.4 million dollars on early AI deployments, largely because of compliance missteps, faulty outputs, and security problems.¬†

Risks like these, Wilkinson said, have left many companies ‚Äúparalyzed‚Äù at the start of their AI journeys. ‚ÄúNo one wants to be the mistake that the news blows up,‚Äù he said.

The public sector faces similar hesitation. Anusha Dandapani, chief of data and AI at the United Nations International Computing Centre (UNICC), said responsible AI in humanitarian work requires strong design, accountability, and models that work across languages and cultures. Many regions lack AI-ready data, she added, which often derails pilots.

AstraZeneca chief architect Wayne Filin-Matthews said the company is embedding agentic AI into its workflows with caution. Across research and development, finance, and IT, the pharmaceutical giant uses a ‚Äúrisk-based approach‚Äù to deployment, prioritizing transparency, security, and regulatory compliance.

AstraZeneca‚Äôs biggest challenge is ensuring that the ‚Äúsemantic kernel,‚Äù the layer connecting AI models to enterprise code, does not compromise security. To address this, the company launched ‚ÄúProject Streamline‚Äù to cut delays from privacy checks and help technical and business teams work together more smoothly.

By the end of the panel, speakers agreed that getting AI right is not just about speed but managing risk with precision. As Nvidia‚Äôs OEM Alliance Manager Matt Hitt put it: ‚ÄúIt's important to start small, measure big, and evaluate‚Äù before scaling.
