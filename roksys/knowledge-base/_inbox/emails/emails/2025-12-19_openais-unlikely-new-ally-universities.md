# New report contradicts AI job fears

View image: (https://media.beehiiv.com/cdn-cgi/image/fit=scale-down,format=auto,onerror=redirect,quality=80/uploads/asset/file/7af32437-600a-4f03-a938-72362c367eb3/ART1__8_.jpg?t=1766124270)
Caption: 

Fears that AI is stealing jobs has been one of the strongest trends of 2025, but there's new evidence trying to debunk that popular narrative.¬†

According to a [recent report from Vanguard](https://corporate.vanguard.com/content/dam/corp/research/pdf/isg_vemo_2026.pdf), wage and job growth has increased over the past two years in roles that are exposed to AI, compared to those that aren‚Äôt. Vanguard measured the growth rate for roles and wages for the top 100 occupations most exposed to AI, and found that annualized growth of jobs saw a 1.7% increase per year since 2019, while annualized ‚Äúreal‚Äù wages saw a growth rate of 3.8%.¬† Comparatively, all other occupations saw an annualized growth rate in jobs of 0.8%, and an annualized ‚Äúreal‚Äù wage increase of 0.7% in the same time period.¬†

The report found that AI systems are enhancing these workers‚Äô productivity, opening them up to higher-value work.¬†

Vanguard's findings sit in stark contrast to broad fears that AI is decimating the job market. While this report may assuage some of those concerns, all it‚Äôs saying is that AI hasn‚Äôt come to replace workers _yet. _Several other reports, however, find that the impact could be dire:¬†

* [MIT and Oak Ridge National Laboratory](https://www.cnbc.com/2025/11/26/mit-study-finds-ai-can-already-replace-11point7percent-of-us-workforce.html) found that AI is already capable of replacing 11.7% of jobs and eating up $1.2 trillion in wages.¬†

* McKinsey, meanwhile, found that AI and robotics could automate activities that [take up 57% of US work hours](https://www.mckinsey.com/mgi/our-research/agents-robots-and-us-skill-partnerships-in-the-age-of-ai).¬†

And the fear of this looming shadow could be impacting workforce morale: [A Pew study](https://www.pewresearch.org/social-trends/2025/02/25/u-s-workers-are-more-worried-than-hopeful-about-future-ai-use-in-the-workplace/) found that more than half of workers surveyed ‚Äì around 52% ‚Äì were worried about AI‚Äôs impact on the workplace, with 32% fearing fewer job opportunities in the long term. A third of employees surveyed reported feeling overwhelmed by the tech.

View image: (https://media.beehiiv.com/cdn-cgi/image/fit=scale-down,format=auto,onerror=redirect,quality=80/uploads/asset/file/9e7d31c7-673b-475f-9c87-cf6c1be7b363/Frame_1410104594.png?t=1765345175)
Caption: 

While most organizations aren‚Äôt firing large swathes of people to replace them with AI agents at this stage, the tech is altering _how_ people work. Large numbers of people are currently figuring out how to integrate AI into their day-to-day jobs, leading employers to invest in reskilling. But not everyone is receptive to this new way of work, and not every role can be reskilled. In October, Accenture let go of 11,000 workers [who couldn‚Äôt be retrained on AI](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/company-just-laid-off-11-123000530.html). What happens to those who can't be reskilled? These workers are largely left out of the rosy AI picture that tech firms are painting of the future. Some, such as Google CEO Sundar Pichai, are [emphasizing that workers will have to adapt](https://fortune.com/2025/12/02/ai-wipes-jobs-google-ceo-sundar-pichai-everyday-people-to-adapt-accordingly-we-have-to-work-through-societal-disruption/) to the ways AI will change the job market.

# ChatGPT apps are buggy but live and ready to try

View image: (https://media.beehiiv.com/cdn-cgi/image/fit=scale-down,format=auto,onerror=redirect,quality=80/uploads/asset/file/a52b5182-33eb-4eda-80aa-610f9c69b503/ART2__6_.jpg?t=1766124267)

[ChatGPT's app directory](https://chatgpt.com/apps/), which lets users interact with external apps directly within OpenAI's popular chatbot, is now live.

The store launched with several dozen apps that users can activate in ChatGPT‚Äôs main interface by pressing the ‚Äú@‚Äù key. OpenAI also opened up app submissions from developers.

While ChatGPT has long offered a GPT store where users can browse custom versions of ChatGPT, the app directory lets third-party apps integrate with the chatbot itself. Featured apps at launch included Adobe Photoshop, Instacart, and Spotify, which let users edit photos, put together a grocery list, or make a playlist ‚Äî at least in theory. As I poked around the app store, I encountered multiple bugs in apps that caused them not to work as intended.

# OpenAI's unlikely new ally: Universities

View image: (https://media.beehiiv.com/cdn-cgi/image/fit=scale-down,format=auto,onerror=redirect,quality=80/uploads/asset/file/2b1bb0c8-4e3e-4197-b73e-9a982cc400e2/ART3__6_.jpg?t=1766124264)
Caption: 

As students use ChatGPT to write their college papers to the chagrin of their professors, OpenAI wants to make its relationship with universities more official.

The company is forging relationships with colleges around the country, having sold more than 700,000 ChatGPT licenses to 35 public universities, [according to Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-18/schools-ink-chatgpt-copilot-deals-with-students-embracing-ai). Students and faculty across 20 campuses used the chatbot more than 14 million times in September, the outlet reported, averaging around 176 uses per month across tasks.¬†

These deals aren‚Äôt the only sign that OpenAI has its eyes on the classroom.¬†

* In November, the company launched a [free version of ChatGPT](http://openai.com/index/chatgpt-for-teachers/) built for K-12 teachers through June 2027, including admin controls for school and district leaders. And teachers are making use of the tech, with around 60% of teachers using some sort of AI tool for their work, [according to Gallup](https://news.gallup.com/poll/691967/three-teachers-weekly-saving-six-weeks-year.aspx).¬†

* On the student side, [ChatGPT introduced ‚Äústudy mode‚Äù](https://openai.com/index/chatgpt-study-mode/) in July, a tool that guides students through challenging homework problems collaboratively rather than just giving them the answers directly.¬†

But whether these schools and universities sanction it or not, students will lean on AI. [A Copyleaks report](https://www.forbes.com/sites/avivalegatt/2025/09/18/90-of-college-students-use-ai-higher-ed-needs-ai-fluency-support-now/) found that 90% of the roughly 1,100 college students surveyed are using AI academically. And even if the tech is prohibited, [AI detection tech is shoddy at best](https://mitsloanedtech.mit.edu/ai/teach/ai-detectors-dont-work/), with false positives and false negatives often gumming up the works.

View image: (https://media.beehiiv.com/cdn-cgi/image/fit=scale-down,format=auto,onerror=redirect,quality=80/uploads/asset/file/9e7d31c7-673b-475f-9c87-cf6c1be7b363/Frame_1410104594.png?t=1765345175)
Caption: 

In partnering with OpenAI and other AI firms, these educators are taking control of something that was otherwise happening right under their noses. Strict, outright bans on the technology will just force students to come up with new and creative ways to cheat. Instead, that control could allow educators to guide students in responsible AI use, leveraging it as a tool rather than a system that does most of the thinking for them. This could also prevent AI overuse, skirting concerns that AI is [eroding critical thinking in young and impressionable minds.](https://time.com/7295195/ai-chatgpt-google-learning-school/)
